## Breaking Down the ORIA Math

Here is
the translation of your whiteboard math into what is actually happening under
the hood:

### 1. The Raw Signal Generation

First, you define the raw target weights generated by your
individual, independent strategies ($A, B, C$, etc.) at time $t$
for the upcoming period $t+1$:

$$u_t^A = w_{t+1}^{A,raw}$$

$$u_t^B = w_{t+1}^{B,raw}$$

You then
stack these into a single matrix to represent the entire ensemble's raw output:

$$U_t = [u_t^A \ u_t^B \ u_t^C]$$

### 2. The Quadratic Optimization (Tracking Error Minimization)

This is where the magic happens. For a given strategy $s$, you aren't just taking the raw weights.
You are running a constrained optimization to find the actual implemented
weights $\bar{w}_t^s$:

$$
\bar{w}_t^s = \arg\min_w \frac{1}{2} (w - u_t^s)^T \Omega_t (w - u_t^s)
$$

- $w$ represents the theoretical optimal weights you are solving for.
- $(w - u_t^s)$ is the difference between your optimized weights and the raw strategy signals.
- $\Omega_t$ is a positive definite penalty matrix—most likely your estimated covariance matrix of asset returns (or idiosyncratic risk).

By minimizing this function, you are mathematically forcing your new
weights to stay as close as possible to the raw alpha signals ($u_t^s$), while heavily penalizing
deviations in assets that are highly volatile or correlated (dictated by $\Omega_t$).

### 3. The Orthogonality Constraint (The "O" in ORIA)

This is the most critical part of your architecture:

$$\text{subject to } F_t^T w = 0$$

- $F_t$ represents a matrix of risk factors (e.g., market beta, sector exposures, momentum, etc.).
- Setting the dot product of your risk factors and your weights to zero ($F_t^T w = 0$) guarantees that your portfolio is strictly orthogonal to those factors.

### Why This Architecture Works

You are effectively isolating pure, uncorrelated alpha. By forcing your weights to be orthogonal to known risk factors, you ensure that profit isn't just a byproduct of a general market rally or accidental sector concentration. You are neutralizing the "noise" (beta) so that the ensemble's true predictive edge (alpha) drives the returns.

## The Allocator Layer

### 1. The Strategy Utility Function (Net Edge)

Before the system decides who gets money, it scores each strategy $s$:

$$
v_t^s = \hat{\eta}_t^s - \lambda_v U_t^s - \lambda_c \hat{c}_t^s
$$

- $\hat{\eta}_t^s$: raw predicted edge / expected return.
- $\lambda_v U_t^s$: uncertainty penalty — discount the edge if predictions are highly variable.
- $\lambda_c \hat{c}_t^s$: expected friction — strategies trading illiquid spreads get penalized.

### 2. The Allocator Optimization (Sleeve Weighting)

Markowitz-style mean-variance, applied at the strategy level:

$$
b_t^* = \arg\max_b \left[ v_t^T b - \frac{\gamma_t}{2} b^T \Sigma_t^{slv} b - \lambda_{turn} \|b - b_{t-1}\|_1 \right]
$$

- $v_t^T b$: maximize total utility of allocated weights.
- $\frac{\gamma_t}{2} b^T \Sigma_t^{slv} b$: overlap penalty (covariance of strategy sleeves).
- $\lambda_{turn} \|b - b_{t-1}\|_1$: L1 turnover penalty — promotes sparsity, neutralizes microscopic churn.

Constraints: $b_t \ge 0$ (no shorting sleeves), $\mathbf{1}^T b \le 1$ (cash is allowed).

### 3. Dynamic Risk Scaling

$$
\rho_t = \mathrm{clip}\!\left( \frac{\sigma^{target,day}}{\hat{\sigma}_t^{mix}} \exp(-\lambda_{stress} \xi_t^{stress}),\, \rho^{min},\, \rho^{max} \right)
$$

- Volatility targeting: scale up if blended risk is below target, down if above.
- Stress override: extreme-stress detector aggressively slashes exposure (panic button).

## The Risk Box (Reality Filter)

$$
w_{t+1}^{rb} = \arg\min_w \left[ \frac{1}{2} (w - w_{t+1}^{alloc})^T \Omega_t (w - w_{t+1}^{alloc}) + \lambda_{rb} \|w - w_t\|_1 \right]
$$

- First term: respect the Allocator's wishes as closely as possible.
- L1 rebalance penalty: prevents over-trading; small Allocator-suggested shifts snap to zero if not worth the friction.
- Hidden constraints: gross/net exposure caps, concentration limits act as hard boundaries.

## The Execution Governor (Final Translation)

Continuous percentages → discrete share orders:

$$
q_{i, t+1}^{tar} = \frac{V_t \cdot w_{i, t+1}^{rb}}{P_{i, t+1}^{ref}}, \qquad \Delta q_{i, t+1}^* = q_{i, t+1}^{tar} - q_{i, t}.
$$

Market-impact, spread-crossing, and participation limits handled at this layer to prevent orders from moving the market.

## Connection to StochasTech

The Phase 1 StochasTech deliverable produces calibrated Heston VaR / ES forecasts on individual tickers. In a hypothetical Phase 3, those forecasts would feed:

- The **Risk Box** as a forward-looking risk model (replacing or augmenting the historical $\Omega_t$).
- The **Allocator's** uncertainty term $\lambda_v U_t^s$ as a model-based volatility input.

That integration is **not** built in v1 — the StochasTech paper merely cites ORIA as the upstream system that motivates better SDE-based risk forecasting.
